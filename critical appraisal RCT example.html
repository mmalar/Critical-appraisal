Background
The Pandemic Context
COVID-19 was declared a global pandemic by the World Health Organisation in March 2020, rapidly overwhelming healthcare systems and triggering an unprecedented international public health emergency. The disease, caused by the SARS-CoV-2 coronavirus, led to millions of hospitalisations and deaths worldwide, disproportionately affecting older adults and those with underlying comorbidities.
Vaccination emerged as the cornerstone strategy to curtail transmission, reduce severe disease, and alleviate healthcare burden. The mRNA-1273 vaccine, developed by Moderna, represented a landmark application of messenger RNA technology — encoding the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein to prime an immune response without using live virus. Its safety and efficacy were evaluated through a rigorous Phase 3 randomised controlled trial, the results of which are the focus of this critical appraisal.

CASP Framework
The CASP RCT Checklist: A Structured Approach
The Critical Appraisal Skills Programme (CASP) RCT checklist provides a standardised, systematic framework for evaluating the rigour and reliability of randomised controlled trial evidence. It is widely used in evidence-based medicine training and clinical guideline development.
Section A — Validity
Are the Results Valid?
The first section of the CASP checklist addresses the fundamental question of internal validity: did the study ask a meaningful question and design itself appropriately to answer it? Screening questions focus on whether the research question was clearly defined, randomisation was properly conducted, and all participants were accounted for throughout the trial.
Q1 — Focused Research Question
Assesses whether the PICO framework (Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome) was clearly stated and clinically meaningful.
Q2 — Randomisation
Evaluates whether assignment to treatment groups was truly random, minimising selection bias and ensuring group comparability.
Q3 — Participant Accounting
Examines whether all enrolled participants were followed up and whether dropouts or losses to follow-up were transparently reported.
CASP Q1
Did the Study Address a Clearly Focused Issue?
Verdict: ✓ Yes
The research question was explicitly and precisely defined using the PICO framework, satisfying the first criterion of the CASP checklist.
PICO Breakdown
  • Population: Adults aged ≥18 years with no prior SARS-CoV-2 infection, including individuals at high risk of severe COVID-19 disease
  • Intervention: Two doses of mRNA-1273 (100 µg per dose) administered intramuscularly 28 days apart
  • Comparison: Matching placebo injection following the same schedule
  • Outcome: Primary — symptomatic COVID-19 at least 14 days after second dose; Secondary — severe COVID-19 disease and safety adverse events
The focused PICO framing ensures the trial results are directly interpretable and actionable for clinicians and policymakers. The inclusion of both efficacy and safety outcomes provides a comprehensive evidence base for regulatory and programmatic decision-making.
CASP Q2
Was the Assignment of Participants Randomised?
Centralised Interactive System
Randomisation was conducted via a centralised, web-based interactive response system, minimising human interference and reducing selection bias. This approach ensured concealment of the allocation sequence from investigators at the point of enrolment.
Stratified Allocation
A 1:1 allocation ratio was employed, with stratification by age group (18–64 years vs ≥65 years) and risk category (high-risk comorbidities vs not). This stratification preserved subgroup balance and enhanced the validity of subgroup analyses.

Verdict: ✓ Yes — Randomisation was appropriately conducted using a robust centralised system with pre-specified stratification, ensuring comparable groups and protecting against allocation bias.
CASP Q3
Were All Participants Accounted for at the End?
Accounting for all enrolled participants is critical to the integrity of an RCT. Unaccounted withdrawals can introduce attrition bias and compromise the validity of effect estimates.
The investigators used both a modified intention-to-treat (mITT) analysis — which included all randomised participants who received at least one dose — and a per-protocol (PP) analysis for participants who completed both doses without major protocol violations. All participant withdrawals and exclusions were transparently reported, satisfying this CASP criterion.

Verdict: ✓ Yes — All 30,420 participants were accounted for, with dropout reasons documented and two complementary analytical approaches applied.
Section B — Methodology
Was the Study Methodologically Sound?
Methodological rigour determines whether the observed outcomes can be reliably attributed to the intervention rather than to bias or confounding. This section of the CASP checklist examines blinding, baseline comparability, consistency of treatment protocols, and completeness of outcome reporting — the pillars of a well-conducted RCT.
Q4–Q6
Blinding — Were participants, investigators, and outcome assessors shielded from knowledge of group allocation?
Q7
Baseline Comparability — Were the vaccine and placebo groups similar in key characteristics at study entry?
Q8
Equal Treatment — Were both groups managed identically apart from the assigned intervention?
Q9
Outcome Reporting — Were all pre-specified primary and secondary outcomes measured and reported?
CASP Q4 & Q5
Were Participants and Investigators Blinded?
Participant Blinding (Q4)
The mRNA-1273 vaccine and matching placebo were formulated to be visually identical in appearance and viscosity. Both were administered using the same injection volume, syringe type, and dosing schedule. Participants were therefore unable to determine which preparation they had received.

✓ Yes — Participant blinding successfully maintained
Investigator Blinding (Q5)
The trial was designed as a double-blind study. Site investigators administering the injections were blinded to group allocation. Only the dispensing pharmacist had access to the randomisation code, and this individual was operationally separated from the clinical team to prevent unblinding.

✓ Yes — Double-blind design with pharmacist firewall maintained blinding integrity
CASP Q6
Were Outcome Assessors Blinded?
Blinding of outcome assessors is essential to prevent ascertainment bias — the risk that knowledge of group allocation may influence how outcomes are measured, recorded, or adjudicated.
Blinded Data Analysts
Statistical analysts and data managers were kept blinded to group allocation throughout the data collection and primary analysis phases of the trial.
Independent DSMB
An independent Data Safety Monitoring Board (DSMB) reviewed unblinded safety data at pre-specified interim points, ensuring participant safety without compromising the overall blinding of the trial.
Endpoint Adjudication
Clinical endpoints, including COVID-19 case adjudication, were assessed by a blinded Endpoint Adjudication Committee to ensure objective and consistent classification of outcomes across all sites.

Verdict: ✓ Yes — Comprehensive blinding of analysts and independent DSMB oversight effectively mitigated ascertainment and detection bias.
CASP Q7
Were Groups Similar at Baseline?
Baseline comparability is a fundamental requirement of a valid RCT. If the groups differ systematically at the outset, it becomes impossible to attribute outcome differences solely to the intervention. In the Baden et al. trial, the vaccine and placebo groups were well-matched across all key characteristics.
Age Distribution
Both groups had comparable age distributions, with approximately 25% of participants aged ≥65 years — a population at elevated risk of severe COVID-19 outcomes.
Sex and Ethnicity
Proportions of male and female participants were balanced. The trial enrolled a racially and ethnically diverse cohort, including Hispanic/Latino (20%), Black or African American (10%), and Asian participants.
Comorbidities and Risk Factors
Participants with high-risk comorbidities — including obesity, diabetes mellitus, and cardiovascular disease — were included and distributed proportionally across both arms, enhancing generalisability.

Verdict: ✓ Yes — Baseline characteristics were well-balanced, supporting the internal validity of the trial.
CASP Q8 & Q9
Equal Treatment and Complete Outcome Reporting
Q8 — Were Groups Treated Equally?
Both the vaccine and placebo groups followed an identical study protocol: the same visit schedule, the same safety monitoring intervals, the same symptom reporting procedures, and the same healthcare access. No co-interventions were differentially applied. Standardised case report forms were used across all sites.
✓ Yes — Protocol standardisation ensured treatment parity.
Q9 — Were All Important Outcomes Reported?
Primary outcome: Symptomatic COVID-19 occurring at least 14 days after the second dose, confirmed by RT-PCR
Secondary outcomes: Severe COVID-19 disease, asymptomatic infection, and safety endpoints including local and systemic adverse events
Safety reporting: Solicited and unsolicited adverse events collected at each visit; serious adverse events adjudicated independently
✓ Yes — All pre-registered outcomes were reported transparently.

Effect Size Interpretation
Interpreting the Measures of Effect
A critical appraisal must go beyond reporting headline efficacy figures. Different measures of effect size tell fundamentally different stories, and understanding their distinctions is essential for informed clinical and policy decision-making.
Relative Risk (RR)
RR = Risk in vaccine group ÷ Risk in placebo group
= (11/15,210) ÷ (185/15,210) ≈ 0.06
Participants in the vaccine group had approximately 6% of the risk of developing COVID-19 compared to those in the placebo group.
Relative Risk Reduction (RRR)
RRR = 1 − RR = 1 − 0.06 ≈ 94%
The vaccine reduced the relative risk of symptomatic COVID-19 by approximately 94%. This is the "headline" figure often cited in media reports.
Absolute Risk Reduction (ARR)
ARR = Risk(placebo) − Risk(vaccine)
= 1.22% − 0.07% ≈ 1.14%
In absolute terms, vaccination prevented approximately 1.14 cases per 100 participants. This measure is dependent on baseline risk in the population.
Number Needed to Vaccinate (NNV)
NNV = 1 ÷ ARR = 1 ÷ 0.0114 ≈ 88
Approximately 88 individuals would need to be vaccinated to prevent one case of symptomatic COVID-19 under trial conditions. NNV rises when baseline population risk is lower.

Important: Relative risk reduction (94%) appears large, but the absolute risk reduction (≈1.1%) and NNV (≈88) are modest, reflecting low baseline incidence during the trial period. Both metrics must be reported for transparent communication.
Visualising Effect Size
RRR vs ARR vs NNV: A Comparative View
The divergence between relative and absolute risk measures is not a flaw in the vaccine — it reflects the mathematical relationship between the two metrics and the baseline incidence of COVID-19 in the trial population. In settings with higher background incidence, the ARR and NNV would be more favourable. This distinction is critical for public health communication and programme planning.
CASP — Precision
How Precise Are the Results?
Statistical precision reflects the degree of uncertainty around a point estimate. Narrow confidence intervals and a highly significant p-value indicate that the observed vaccine efficacy of 94.1% is a reliable estimate, unlikely to have arisen by chance.
Key Precision Indicators
  • 95% CI: 89.3–96.8% — a narrow interval, indicating high precision
  • P-value: < 0.001 — highly statistically significant
  • Consistency: Efficacy remained above 90% across all pre-specified subgroups, including older adults (≥65 years) and those with high-risk comorbidities
Interpreting the Confidence Interval
The 95% confidence interval (89.3–96.8%) indicates that if the study were repeated 100 times under identical conditions, we would expect the true vaccine efficacy to fall within this range on 95 occasions. The lower bound of 89.3% still represents a clinically meaningful and substantial protective effect.
Subgroup analyses — by age, ethnicity, comorbidity status, and serostatus — demonstrated consistent efficacy, strengthening confidence in the robustness of the primary finding.
Safety Profile
Adverse Events and Safety Data
Safety evaluation is integral to any vaccine trial appraisal. Adverse events were systematically collected through electronic diaries and clinic visits, with solicited data gathered in the seven days following each injection.
Local Reactions
Injection site pain, erythema, and swelling were reported more frequently in the vaccine group following both doses. Most reactions were mild to moderate in severity and resolved within a few days. Severe injection-site reactions occurred in fewer than 1% of vaccine recipients.
Systemic Reactions
Fatigue, headache, myalgia, arthralgia, and chills were significantly more common in the vaccine group, particularly after the second dose. These reactogenic symptoms are consistent with an active immune response and were generally transient.
Serious Adverse Events
The incidence of serious adverse events was low and comparable between groups. No vaccine-related deaths were reported. However, the median follow-up period of approximately two months limits the detection of rare or delayed adverse events.

Limitation: With a relatively short follow-up duration, rare adverse events occurring beyond two months post-vaccination — such as myocarditis signals identified in post-authorisation surveillance — could not be detected within the trial period.
Benefit-Risk Balance
Benefits vs Harms: Overall Assessment
Benefits
  • Vaccine efficacy of 94.1% against symptomatic COVID-19 (95% CI: 89.3–96.8%)
  • 100% efficacy against severe COVID-19 in the trial population
  • Consistent protection across age groups, ethnicities, and comorbidity categories
  • Robust immunogenicity with high seroconversion rates following both doses
Harms
  • Higher rates of solicited local and systemic adverse events vs placebo
  • Majority mild to moderate, self-limiting within days
  • Rare adverse events (e.g., anaphylaxis) not fully characterised within trial setting
  • Short-term follow-up limits detection of delayed or long-term safety signals

Overall Assessment: The benefits of mRNA-1273 vaccination substantially outweigh its risks under the conditions of the trial. The safety profile is acceptable and consistent with regulatory authorisation standards. Ongoing post-authorisation pharmacovigilance is essential.
Section D — Applicability
Can the Results Be Applied in Practice?
The fourth domain of the CASP appraisal asks whether the trial findings are applicable to the local context. For Malaysian healthcare professionals, this question requires consideration of participant representativeness, healthcare infrastructure, vaccine storage logistics, and the broader public health environment at the time of implementation.
Population Representativeness
The trial included Asian participants, though they constituted a small subgroup. Age distribution, comorbidity profile, and demographic diversity in the trial are broadly comparable to the Malaysian adult population at elevated COVID-19 risk.
Healthcare Context
Malaysia's dual public-private healthcare system has the infrastructure to support large-scale vaccination programmes. The availability of trained personnel and cold chain logistics for 2–8°C storage is feasible within the national immunisation delivery framework.
Ongoing Surveillance
Given the limited trial follow-up and small Asian subgroup sizes, local post-authorisation pharmacovigilance is essential to monitor for rare adverse events and to assess real-world effectiveness in the Malaysian population over time.
Local Applicability
Applicability to Malaysia: Opportunities and Limitations
Factors Supporting Applicability
  • Asian participants were included in the trial, providing some direct ethnic representation
  • High-risk groups (elderly, comorbid) featured in the trial mirror Malaysian priority populations
  • mRNA-1273 storage at 2–8°C is achievable in standard Malaysian cold chain infrastructure
  • Results are consistent with WHO guidance supporting mRNA vaccine deployment in low- and middle-income settings
Limitations to Consider
  • Asian subgroup in the trial was relatively small; local effect estimates may differ
  • Trial conducted exclusively in the United States — population-level immunity, prior exposure, and healthcare-seeking behaviour may differ in Malaysia
  • Variant landscape at time of trial (ancestral strain) differs from subsequently circulating variants (Delta, Omicron)
  • Ongoing national pharmacovigilance (e.g., NPRA) is required to complement trial data
Value to Practice
Clinical and Public Health Value
The mRNA-1273 trial provides high-quality evidence supporting large-scale vaccination programmes. Its findings directly informed emergency use authorisations and national immunisation policies in multiple countries, including Malaysia's National COVID-19 Immunisation Programme (PICK).
Reduces Morbidity and Mortality
High efficacy against symptomatic and severe COVID-19 translates into substantial reductions in hospitalisations, ICU admissions, and COVID-19-associated deaths at the population level — particularly among high-risk groups.
Supports Vaccination Programme Design
Robust subgroup data enables prioritisation decisions — ensuring older adults, those with comorbidities, and high-exposure occupational groups receive early access to vaccination.
Feasible for Implementation
The 2–8°C storage requirement (significantly less demanding than early mRNA-1273 formulations requiring −20°C) enhances distribution feasibility in primary care and community settings across Malaysia.
Summary
Critical Appraisal Summary: Overall Verdict
The Baden et al. (2021) Phase 3 RCT represents one of the most rigorously conducted vaccine trials in modern medical history. Across all CASP domains — validity, methodology, results, and applicability — the trial meets or exceeds the standards expected of high-quality RCT evidence. Its findings provide a compelling evidence base for the inclusion of mRNA-1273 in national COVID-19 immunisation programmes.
Strengths
Study Strengths
Large Sample Size
30,420 participants provides substantial statistical power to detect clinically meaningful differences and supports subgroup analyses.
Rigorous Double-Blind Design
Comprehensive blinding at participant, investigator, and assessor levels minimises performance, detection, and reporting biases.
Multicentre Enrolment
Recruitment across multiple geographically diverse sites enhances external validity and reduces the influence of site-specific confounding.
Exceptional Efficacy
A vaccine efficacy of 94.1% substantially exceeds the WHO minimum threshold of 50% for COVID-19 vaccine authorisation.
Limitations
Study Limitations and Caveats
Short Follow-Up Duration
The median follow-up of approximately two months is insufficient to characterise the durability of vaccine-induced immunity, long-term efficacy waning, or delayed adverse events. Post-authorisation surveillance data subsequently demonstrated the need for booster doses as immunity waned.
Underrepresentation of Some Subgroups
Although the trial included racially diverse participants, Asian and other non-White ethnic subgroups were relatively small, limiting the precision of subgroup-specific efficacy estimates. Paediatric populations were excluded entirely.
Ancestral Strain Specificity
The trial was conducted against the ancestral SARS-CoV-2 strain. Vaccine effectiveness against subsequently emerging variants of concern (Delta, Omicron) required separate real-world effectiveness studies and informed subsequent bivalent booster development.
Rare Adverse Events Undetected
With a sample of 30,420 participants and short follow-up, adverse events occurring at rates below approximately 1 in 10,000 could not be reliably detected. Post-marketing surveillance subsequently identified signals including rare myocarditis cases, particularly in younger males.

Conclusion: Evidence Supports Implementation
The Phase 3 RCT by Baden et al. (2021) provides compelling, high-quality evidence for the safety and efficacy of the mRNA-1273 SARS-CoV-2 vaccine. Applying the CASP framework reveals a methodologically rigorous trial with valid outcomes, precise results, and a favourable benefit-risk balance.
Vaccine efficacy of 94.1% against symptomatic COVID-19 — consistent across age groups, ethnicities, and comorbidity categories — substantially supports its integration into national immunisation programmes. For the Malaysian context, the evidence is broadly applicable, subject to ongoing pharmacovigilance, monitoring of variant-specific effectiveness, and attention to small subgroup limitations.
Critical appraisal is not about finding reasons to dismiss evidence — it is about understanding its quality, scope, and limits, so that it can be applied wisely in the real world.
